DOC_ID: RBI_DSCR_001
CATEGORY: rbi_guidelines
TITLE: RBI DSCR Norms for Term Loans
---
The Reserve Bank of India mandates that Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR) should be a minimum of 1.25x for project finance and term loans. A DSCR below 1.0x means the borrower cannot service debt from operating cash flows. Banks are required to stress-test DSCR at higher interest rates (+200bps). For infrastructure projects, minimum DSCR of 1.10x is acceptable under Harmonised Master List guidelines. RBI circular DBOD.BP.BC.No.110/21.04.048/2013-14 requires banks to monitor DSCR quarterly for large exposures above Rs 5 crore. Persistent DSCR below 1.0x for two consecutive quarters warrants classification as Special Mention Account (SMA-1). Average DSCR over loan tenure should be at least 1.2x even if individual year DSCR dips slightly.

DOC_ID: RBI_NPA_002
CATEGORY: rbi_guidelines
TITLE: RBI NPA Classification and Provisioning Norms
---
As per RBI Master Circular on Income Recognition, Asset Classification and Provisioning (IRACP), a Non-Performing Asset (NPA) is a loan where interest or principal has been overdue for more than 90 days. Sub-standard assets: NPA for less than 12 months, provision requirement 15%. Doubtful assets (1 year): 25% provision on secured portion, 100% on unsecured. Loss assets: 100% provision. Banks must disclose NPA ratios in annual reports. RBI's Prompt Corrective Action (PCA) framework triggers when Net NPA exceeds 6% of net advances. GNPA ratio above 10% triggers restrictions on dividends and branch expansion. Special Mention Account (SMA-0): overdue 1-30 days. SMA-1: 31-60 days. SMA-2: 61-90 days. Proactive monitoring required at SMA stage.

DOC_ID: RBI_COLLATERAL_003
CATEGORY: rbi_guidelines
TITLE: RBI Guidelines on Collateral and Security
---
RBI guidelines under SARFAESI Act 2002 allow banks to enforce collateral without court intervention for NPAs above Rs 1 lakh. Loan-to-Value (LTV) ratio norms: residential property maximum 80% LTV for loans up to Rs 30 lakh, 75% for Rs 30-75 lakh, 75% above Rs 75 lakh. For commercial real estate, maximum LTV is 65%. Movable assets like machinery and stock: typically 50-60% LTV due to realisability discount. RBI requires independent valuation every 3 years for property collateral. Third-party guarantees are acceptable as credit enhancement but not primary collateral. Collateral must be insured and banks must ensure first and exclusive charge on the security.

DOC_ID: RBI_EXPOSURE_004
CATEGORY: rbi_guidelines
TITLE: RBI Large Exposure Framework
---
RBI's Large Exposure Framework (LEF) limits a bank's exposure to a single counterparty to 20% of its Tier 1 capital, and to a group of connected counterparties to 25% of Tier 1 capital. For systemically important banks (D-SIBs), the limit is 15% for single counterparty. Exposures exceeding 10% of Tier 1 capital must be reported to RBI quarterly. Concentration risk in a single sector should not exceed 15% of total advances. Banks must maintain sector-wise NPA analysis and flag sectors with rising stress.

DOC_ID: RBI_INTEREST_RATE_005
CATEGORY: rbi_guidelines
TITLE: RBI Interest Rate Setting and MCLR Framework
---
RBI's Marginal Cost of Funds based Lending Rate (MCLR) framework replaced base rate in April 2016. All new floating rate retail and MSME loans are linked to external benchmarks (repo rate, 91-day T-bill, or 3-month MIBOR) since October 2019. MCLR is reset monthly. Risk premium (spread) over MCLR depends on borrower's credit profile. Internal credit rating must justify the spread. Penal interest for default must be disclosed transparently. Interest should stop accruing on NPAs (cash basis recognition only).

DOC_ID: RBI_KYC_006
CATEGORY: rbi_guidelines
TITLE: RBI KYC and Due Diligence Requirements
---
RBI Master Direction on KYC requires Customer Due Diligence (CDD) for all borrowers. Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) required for: politically exposed persons, high-risk geographies, complex ownership structures. For corporate borrowers, banks must identify and verify Ultimate Beneficial Owner (UBO) owning more than 25% equity or effective control. Annual review mandatory for high-risk customers. Source of funds and wealth must be documented for large loan applications. AML checks via CERSAI registry for collateral encumbrances. Suspicious Transaction Reports (STRs) must be filed with FIU-India within 7 days.

DOC_ID: GST_COMPLIANCE_007
CATEGORY: gst_regulations
TITLE: GST Compliance for Credit Appraisal
---
GST 2A-3B reconciliation mismatch is a critical red flag in credit appraisal. GSTR-2A reflects purchases as reported by suppliers; GSTR-3B is self-declared. A mismatch indicates either inflated Input Tax Credit (ITC) claims (fraud) or revenue suppression. GST Council circular 26/26/2017 mandates monthly reconciliation. Banks must obtain 12-month GST returns during credit appraisal. A mismatch exceeding 10% of declared turnover is considered significant. Revenue declared in GST returns should closely match banking turnover (within 15% tolerance). Persistent mismatches may indicate circular transactions or shell company activity. RBI has directed banks to verify GST returns as part of KYC for loans above Rs 2 crore.

DOC_ID: GST_EVASION_008
CATEGORY: gst_regulations
TITLE: GST Evasion Signals in Corporate Credit
---
Common GST evasion indicators in corporate credit appraisal: (1) Turnover declared in GSTR-1 significantly lower than banking transactions. (2) ITC claims without corresponding GSTR-2A entries. (3) Multiple GST registrations for same entity in same state without valid reason. (4) Zero-rated exports without corresponding foreign exchange inflows per FEMA records. (5) Sudden spike in turnover in final quarter before loan application. Banks must obtain Form 26AS and cross-verify with GST turnover. Any Rs 50 lakh discrepancy between IT and GST turnover warrants enhanced due diligence.

DOC_ID: MCA_COMPLIANCE_009
CATEGORY: mca_regulations
TITLE: MCA Filing Requirements and Defaults
---
Companies registered under Companies Act 2013 must file annual returns (MGT-7) and financial statements (AOC-4) with Ministry of Corporate Affairs (MCA) within 60 days of AGM. ROC late filing attracts additional fees: Rs 100 per day for small companies. Companies with consistent filing defaults may face disqualification of directors under Section 164(2). Credit analysts must verify MCA21 portal for: filing status, charge creation/satisfaction, director disqualifications, and pending litigations. A company with unfiled returns for 2+ years is classified as a dormant company. Banks must check for any pending winding-up petitions filed at NCLT.

DOC_ID: MCA_CHARGE_010
CATEGORY: mca_regulations
TITLE: MCA Charge Registration and Encumbrances
---
All charges created on company assets must be registered with MCA within 30 days of creation under Section 77 of Companies Act 2013. Failure to register makes the charge void against the liquidator. Banks must search MCA21 portal to check existing charges before disbursement. Multiple charges on the same asset from different lenders indicates consortium lending requiring NOC. CERSAI records all charges on immovable property and must be checked independently. Undisclosed charges are a fraud signal and violate covenants.

DOC_ID: WORKING_CAPITAL_011
CATEGORY: credit_norms
TITLE: Working Capital Assessment - Tandon Committee Norms
---
Tandon Committee norms (RBI 1975, still applicable) govern working capital finance in India. Maximum Permissible Bank Finance (MPBF) Method 2: 75% of (current assets - current liabilities excluding bank borrowings). Current Ratio minimum of 1.17x required under MPBF method 2. Working capital cycle analysis: Debtor days + Inventory days - Creditor days = Net working capital cycle. Industry benchmarks: Manufacturing 60-90 days cycle, Trading 30-45 days, Services 15-30 days. Stock statements monthly and book debt statements quarterly required for limits above Rs 10 crore.

DOC_ID: MSME_CREDIT_012
CATEGORY: credit_norms
TITLE: MSME Credit Policy and Priority Sector Norms
---
Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises (MSME) classification (2020): Micro - investment up to Rs 1 crore, turnover up to Rs 5 crore. Small - investment up to Rs 10 crore, turnover up to Rs 50 crore. Medium - investment up to Rs 50 crore, turnover up to Rs 250 crore. Priority Sector Lending (PSL) target: 40% of Adjusted Net Bank Credit for all banks. MSME sub-target: 7.5% of ANBC. Collateral-free loans up to Rs 20 lakh under CGTMSE guarantee scheme. Emergency Credit Line Guarantee Scheme (ECLGS) provides 100% guarantee for COVID-affected MSMEs.

DOC_ID: CREDIT_MONITORING_013
CATEGORY: credit_norms
TITLE: Credit Monitoring and Early Warning Signals
---
RBI's Early Warning System (EWS) requires banks to identify stress before NPA classification. Key EWS signals: (1) Frequent requests for loan restructuring. (2) Cheque bouncing in account. (3) Increase in creditor days without corresponding sales growth. (4) Diversion of funds to group companies. (5) Promoter pledging more than 50% of shareholding. (6) Resignation of statutory auditor mid-year. (7) Delay in submission of QIS or stock statements. (8) Large cash withdrawals inconsistent with business profile. Consortium lenders must share EWS data. Annual credit reviews mandatory for accounts above Rs 5 crore.

DOC_ID: GOVERNANCE_RPT_014
CATEGORY: governance
TITLE: Related Party Transactions and Fund Diversion Risk
---
Related Party Transactions (RPTs) under Companies Act 2013 and SEBI LODR regulations require board and shareholder approval above certain thresholds. Large RPTs can indicate fund diversion from the borrowing entity to promoter entities. Key red flags: (1) Loans to subsidiaries exceeding 60% of paid-up capital without board approval. (2) Property purchase from promoter group at non-market prices. (3) Management fees paid to promoter entities at inflated rates. (4) Guarantees given for group company borrowings without disclosure. RPTs exceeding 10% of annual revenue warrant enhanced scrutiny.

DOC_ID: GOVERNANCE_AUDIT_015
CATEGORY: governance
TITLE: Auditor Qualifications and Emphasis of Matter
---
Auditor qualifications in financial statements are serious red flags for credit analysis. Types of audit opinions: Unqualified (clean), Qualified (except for certain matters), Adverse (statements not fairly presented), Disclaimer (unable to form opinion). Emphasis of Matter paragraphs highlight significant uncertainties - going concern doubts, material uncertainties in litigation, significant related party dependencies. Statutory auditor resignation mid-year is a critical red flag. Banks must read full audit report, not just summary opinion. Key qualification areas: revenue recognition disputes, inventory valuation, receivables recoverability, contingent liability adequacy.

DOC_ID: GOVERNANCE_PROMOTER_016
CATEGORY: governance
TITLE: Promoter Due Diligence and Succession Planning
---
Promoter quality assessment is critical in Indian credit markets where promoter-managed firms dominate. Key evaluation criteria: (1) Track record across economic cycles. (2) Personal wealth relative to loan - skin in the game. (3) History of defaults in CIBIL or CRILC. (4) Criminal background via court records. (5) Key-man risk - company dependent on single promoter without succession plan. (6) Family disputes among promoters creating management paralysis. Positive signals: second-generation professional management, independent board with domain experts. Negative signals: frequent auditor changes, lifestyle incompatible with reported income.

DOC_ID: FRAUD_DETECTION_017
CATEGORY: fraud_prevention
TITLE: Credit Fraud Detection Framework
---
RBI's Framework for dealing with Loan Frauds (2016) mandates banks to report fraud above Rs 1 crore to CBI or Police and RBI within 7 days of classification. Common credit fraud types: (1) Kite flying - circular transactions between group companies to inflate revenue. (2) Double financing - same collateral pledged with multiple banks. (3) Diversion of term loan funds to working capital or personal use. (4) Inflation of stock statements for working capital enhancement. (5) Fake invoices for ITC benefit and revenue inflation. CRILC tracks borrower defaults across all lenders.

DOC_ID: FRAUD_WILFUL_018
CATEGORY: fraud_prevention
TITLE: Wilful Default Classification and Consequences
---
RBI's Master Circular on Wilful Defaulters defines wilful default as: (1) Default despite adequate cash flows and net worth. (2) Diversion or siphoning of funds. (3) Disposal of collateral without bank consent. (4) Removal of assets securing the loan. Once classified as wilful defaulter, no new credit facility can be sanctioned for 5 years. Promoters of wilful defaulters cannot serve as directors. Joint lenders forum must be convened within 30 days of SMA-2 classification for accounts above Rs 100 crore.

DOC_ID: IBC_INSOLVENCY_019
CATEGORY: governance
TITLE: Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code 2016
---
Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code (IBC) 2016 provides time-bound resolution of 180 days extendable to 270 days for stressed assets. Financial creditors (banks) can trigger CIRP (Corporate Insolvency Resolution Process) when default exceeds Rs 1 crore. Resolution Professional takes management control during CIRP. Successful resolution requires 66% vote by Committee of Creditors by value. Haircuts in IBC resolution have ranged from 30% to 95% of admitted claims. Pre-pack insolvency for MSMEs allows faster resolution. Key sectors with high IBC filings: infrastructure, EPC, real estate, steel, telecom.

DOC_ID: SECTOR_MANUFACTURING_020
CATEGORY: sector_intelligence
TITLE: Manufacturing Sector Credit Risk Analysis
---
Indian manufacturing sector credit risk profile: Capital-intensive with high fixed cost burden. Key ratios: asset turnover should exceed 1.5x; EBITDA margins typically 8-15%; working capital cycle 60-90 days. Risks: commodity price volatility, import competition, power cost inflation, labour disputes. Positive signals: long-term supply contracts, export revenue diversification, PLI (Production-Linked Incentive) scheme participation. Sector-specific risks: Auto ancillary - EV transition risk; Textiles - global demand cycles; Pharma - USFDA compliance risk. Working capital finance dominates. Term loans for capex must align with product lifecycle.

DOC_ID: SECTOR_REAL_ESTATE_021
CATEGORY: sector_intelligence
TITLE: Real Estate Sector Credit Risk
---
Real estate is a high-risk sector for bank credit in India due to cyclical demand, project completion risk, regulatory approvals uncertainty, and high leverage. RBI has restricted bank exposure to real estate developers to 15% of total advances. RERA (Real Estate Regulation and Development Act) 2016 mandates 70% of collections deposited in escrow account for project completion. Key risks: slow project completion, diversion of buyer collections, unsold inventory buildup. Positive signals: RERA registration, strong pre-sales, diversified project portfolio.

DOC_ID: SECTOR_NBFC_022
CATEGORY: sector_intelligence
TITLE: NBFC Sector Credit Risk and RBI Oversight
---
Non-Banking Financial Companies (NBFCs) face enhanced regulatory scrutiny post-IL&FS crisis. RBI's Scale-Based Regulation (SBR) 2022 classifies NBFCs into Base, Middle, Upper, and Top layers based on size and risk. Capital Adequacy Ratio minimum 15% for deposit-taking NBFCs, 10% for non-deposit. Stressed NBFC indicators: high cost of funds (above 10%), rising NPAs in AUM, inability to roll over commercial paper, rating downgrades. Credit analysis must cover AUM portfolio quality, collection efficiency, and borrowing cost trajectory.

DOC_ID: CIBIL_CREDIT_026
CATEGORY: credit_norms
TITLE: CIBIL and Credit Bureau Assessment for Corporates
---
TransUnion CIBIL, Equifax, CRIF High Mark, and Experian are licensed credit bureaus in India. Corporate credit scores reflect consortium lending exposure, defaults, and payment track record. CRILC (Central Repository of Information on Large Credits) tracks exposures above Rs 5 crore across all lenders - must be checked for all corporate borrowers. DPD (Days Past Due) more than 90 in last 24 months is a negative signal. Written-off accounts in credit bureau indicate lender gave up recovery - serious red flag. Restructured accounts show in credit bureau and indicate past stress.

DOC_ID: CAPITAL_ADEQUACY_027
CATEGORY: rbi_guidelines
TITLE: Capital Adequacy and Basel III Norms
---
Basel III capital adequacy norms as adopted by RBI: Minimum Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1): 5.5%; Additional Tier 1: 1.5%; Tier 2: 2%; Capital Conservation Buffer: 2.5%; Total minimum CAR: 11.5%. D-SIBs (SBI, HDFC, ICICI) face additional surcharge. Risk-weighted assets: 100% for corporate loans, 75% for SME and retail, 35% for residential mortgage. Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR): 100% - sufficient HQLA to survive 30-day stress. Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR): 100%.

DOC_ID: RESTRUCTURING_028
CATEGORY: rbi_guidelines
TITLE: RBI Loan Restructuring and Resolution Frameworks
---
RBI has issued multiple restructuring frameworks post-COVID: Resolution Framework 1.0 (2020) and 2.0 (2021) for COVID-impacted borrowers. One-Time Restructuring allowed without NPA classification if borrower was standard as of reference date. Implementation conditions: minimum 10% additional provision; promoter skin-in-the-game requirement; independent credit evaluation for accounts above Rs 1,500 crore. Restructured accounts must be monitored quarterly. Evergreening (rolling over bad loans without recognising stress) is a regulatory violation.

DOC_ID: DIGITAL_LENDING_029
CATEGORY: rbi_guidelines
TITLE: RBI Digital Lending Guidelines 2022
---
RBI's Digital Lending Guidelines (September 2022) regulate Lending Service Providers (LSPs) and First Loss Default Guarantee (FLDG) arrangements. Key provisions: loan disbursement and repayment must flow through borrower's bank account only. Regulated entities cannot partner with LSPs charging exorbitant interest rates. Key Fact Statement (KFS) mandatory - must include APR, all fees, and recovery practices. FLDG arrangement: LSP bears first loss up to 5% of loan book. Data collection limited to purpose. AI and ML models for credit assessment must be explainable with adequate disclosure.

DOC_ID: SECTOR_INFRA_030
CATEGORY: sector_intelligence
TITLE: Infrastructure Project Finance Credit Norms
---
Infrastructure lending requires specialised analysis: project completion risk, traffic and revenue risk, regulatory risk, force majeure. Key framework: Build-Operate-Transfer (BOT), Hybrid Annuity Model (HAM), Engineering-Procurement-Construction (EPC). DSCR minimum 1.2x for highways; 1.1x for projects with government annuity providing revenue certainty. Concession agreements are primary credit support. Waterfall mechanism: O&M costs, then senior debt service, then maintenance reserve, then subordinated debt, then equity. Common risks: cost overruns typically 20-30%, delay-related revenue loss, land acquisition failures.
